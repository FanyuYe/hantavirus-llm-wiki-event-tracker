# Hondius Hantavirus Outbreak Timeline 2026

## Current Snapshot

As of the latest verified source review captured on 2026-05-12 AEST, the outbreak is linked to the Dutch-flagged expedition vessel _m/v Hondius_ after a South Atlantic itinerary that departed Ushuaia, Argentina, on 2026-04-01. The current strongest official global case-count baseline remains WHO DON600: as of 2026-05-08 there were 8 cases, including 3 deaths, with 6 laboratory-confirmed Andes virus cases and 2 probable cases. Newer country-level sources report additional or reclassified positives after repatriation, including a positive French passenger in deteriorating/critical condition, a positive asymptomatic U.S. passenger admitted to Nebraska's biocontainment unit, and one provisional positive Spanish passenger awaiting confirmatory testing. These later reports should not yet be summed into a new global count until WHO, ECDC, or national authorities reconcile overlap with the earlier probable/suspected cases. [WHO DON600](sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-don600-2026-05-08.md) [France Reuters](sources/hondius-hantavirus-france-positive-reuters-2026-05-11.md) [UNMC U.S. arrival](sources/hondius-hantavirus-unmc-us-arrival-2026-05-11.md) [Spain provisional positive](sources/hondius-hantavirus-spain-provisional-positive-2026-05-11.md)

The core unresolved question is how exposure occurred. WHO says the working hypothesis is that case 1 most probably acquired infection before boarding through environmental exposure during activities in Argentina and Chile, while current evidence points to subsequent human-to-human transmission onboard. New science reporting on initial sequencing from the Swiss patient supports Andes virus without signs of a new reassorted or unusually divergent strain, but broader transmission-chain reconstruction still depends on sequencing from additional cases. The operational focus has shifted to completed Tenerife evacuation, national quarantine/testing, crew/ship management, and 42-day active monitoring rules across destination countries. ## Standing Interpretation

The incident is not a routine cruise-ship gastrointestinal outbreak. It is a rare severe respiratory illness cluster caused by hantavirus, now identified by WHO and ECDC as Andes virus. The epidemiological concern is narrower than COVID-style respiratory spread but higher than most hantavirus clusters because Andes virus can rarely transmit between people through close and prolonged contact. [WHO response](sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-response-2026-05-07.md) [ECDC response](sources/hondius-hantavirus-ecdc-response-activated-2026-05-06.md)

The main control strategy visible in the official record is coordinated case care, evacuation of symptomatic or high-risk contacts, onboard medical monitoring, laboratory testing, contact tracing across countries, and controlled arrival/disembarkation in the Canary Islands. [WHO DON](sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-don599-2026-05-07.md) [WHO response](sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-response-2026-05-07.md) [Spain La Moncloa](sources/hondius-hantavirus-spain-lamoncloa-2026-05-07.md)

## Open Questions

- Exact exposure source: WHO's working hypothesis points to pre-boarding environmental exposure in Argentina/Chile for case 1 followed by onboard human-to-human transmission, but sequencing and epidemiological investigations remain ongoing.
- Final reconciled public case count: WHO DON600 confirms 8 cases, 6 confirmed and 2 probable, but post-repatriation country reports now include French/U.S. positives and a Spanish provisional positive that need official global reconciliation.
- Tenerife execution: media reports say evacuation finished and MV Hondius departed for Rotterdam, but a final official all-passenger completion statement has not yet been captured.
- Passenger and crew follow-up outcomes: repatriation, quarantine, testing, and any later cases after the Canary Islands arrival need daily monitoring, especially the French critical case, the U.S. asymptomatic positive, and the Spanish provisional positive.
- Clinical outcomes: latest official sources do not yet resolve the outcomes of the ICU patient in South Africa, the Switzerland case, or the three medically transferred people.
