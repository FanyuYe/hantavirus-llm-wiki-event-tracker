## Situation Assessment

The outbreak has progressed from an initially unexplained severe respiratory illness cluster to a confirmed Andes hantavirus event with international contact-tracing implications. The earliest known illness onset was 2026-04-06, the first death occurred aboard on 2026-04-11, and WHO was formally notified on 2026-05-02. [WHO DON](../sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-don599-2026-05-07.md)

For severity, the critical facts are 3 deaths and severe respiratory disease in multiple cases, including rapid progression to pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and shock as described by WHO and ECDC. [WHO DON](../sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-don599-2026-05-07.md) [ECDC assessment](../sources/hondius-hantavirus-ecdc-assessment-2026-05-06.md)

For spread risk, the key distinction is that most hantaviruses do not spread person to person, but Andes virus can do so rarely. Official sources still frame public risk as low because spread requires close and prolonged contact and because control measures are being applied. [WHO response](../sources/hondius-hantavirus-who-response-2026-05-07.md) [ECDC assessment](../sources/hondius-hantavirus-ecdc-assessment-2026-05-06.md)
